Historical Weapons

Historical weapons in Agusan del Sur are a significant part of the region’s rich cultural and martial history, particularly among its indigenous groups such as the Manobo, Higaonon, and T'boli peoples. These weapons, traditionally crafted from local materials, were used for various purposes including self-defense, hunting, and in the context of warfare. The weaponry from Agusan del Sur reflects the deep connection between the people and their environment, as well as their beliefs in protection, honor, and ancestral spirits.


One of the most notable historical weapons in Agusan del Sur is the sword, often called the "kampilan" or "bolo". The kampilan, a single-edged sword, was primarily used by the Manobo and Higaonon peoples in the region. Its blade is typically long and curved, designed for cutting, slashing, and combat. The handle of the kampilan often features intricate carvings, with designs representing ancestral spirits or natural elements, showcasing the artistry and cultural significance of the weapon. The bolo, a type of machete-like blade, was widely used in everyday life for tasks such as clearing land, cutting wood, and harvesting crops, but it also served as a tool for defense during conflicts.

Further Discoveries of Historical Weapons

In addition to these weapons, spears and shields were also integral to the martial culture of the indigenous peoples of Agusan del Sur. The spear, often referred to as "sundang", was typically used for both hunting and warfare. It featured a long wooden shaft with a sharp iron or steel tip. Spears were often carried by warriors during battles and were believed to possess spiritual power, symbolizing strength and protection. The shield, made from woven bamboo or leather, was used to protect warriors during combat. Shields often had symbolic decorations, reflecting the warrior’s identity, status, and connection to the community’s spiritual beliefs.

These historical weapons, while no longer used in the same way today, continue to serve as symbols of the region’s cultural heritage. They represent the resilience, bravery, and resourcefulness of the indigenous peoples of Agusan del Sur. Today, many of these weapons are preserved as artifacts in museums or cultural centers, offering a glimpse into the martial history and traditions of the region.
