Wooden Sculptures

Carved wood sculptures in Davao Oriental are a testament to the artistic traditions and rich cultural heritage of the indigenous communities in the region, particularly the Mandaya, Mansaka, and Kalagan peoples. These wood carvings, crafted from locally sourced hardwoods such as mahogany, narra, and acacia, are not only artistic expressions but also hold significant spiritual and cultural meaning. The tradition of wood carving in Davao Oriental is deeply connected to the people’s relationship with their environment, their ancestors, and their spiritual beliefs.


The Mandaya people, who are well-known for their skilled craftsmanship, create intricate wood sculptures that often depict ancestral spirits, mythological creatures, and nature-based motifs. These carvings are typically used in rituals and ceremonies or as decorations in homes and communal spaces. Some of the most famous carvings include ritual masks, spirit figures, and carved wooden totems, which are believed to provide protection, blessings, and guidance from the ancestral spirits.

Further Discoveries of Wooden Sculptures

The Mansaka and Kalagan peoples also engage in wood carving, with each community showcasing unique techniques and styles. For example, Mansaka carvings often feature human figures and animal representations that are important in their ceremonial practices, while Kalagan carvings might focus more on symbolic representations tied to the region's natural elements, such as trees, rivers, and animals. These sculptures serve not only as a means of artistic expression but also as visual representations of tribal identity and cultural beliefs.

Carving is a painstaking and time-consuming process that involves selecting the right wood, drying it, and then carefully shaping it using traditional tools. The artisans use simple hand tools like chisels and knives to carve the intricate details that give the sculptures their unique texture and character. Today, these wood sculptures continue to be highly valued as cultural treasures. Many are sold in local markets or featured in cultural festivals, helping to preserve the traditions of Davao Oriental's indigenous peoples while providing a livelihood for skilled artisans. These carved wooden figures are more than just artwork—they are a link between past and present, embodying the spirituality, identity, and history of Davao Oriental’s indigenous communities.
